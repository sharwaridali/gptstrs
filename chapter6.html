Chapter 6: The Distant Horizon

The morning sun filtered through the curtains of their cozy bnb, casting a warm glow on the wooden floors. The group gathered around the rustic kitchen table, enjoying a hearty breakfast of scrambled eggs, toast, and freshly brewed coffee.

John grinned as he raised his mug. “Alrigh! kids. Today’s agenda: Perranporth Beach, a quick stop at the Perranzabuloe Museum, then off to Tintagel Castle. After that, we’ll head back to catch *Twelfth Night* at Minack Theatre. Sound good?”

The table erupted in cheers, the excitement palpable as they dug into their meal.

Hannah’s eyes lit up. “Absolutely! I’ve always wanted to see Tintagel. The legends, the ruins… it’s all so romantic.”

Gareth raised an eyebrow. “As long as there’s food involved at some point, I’m in.”

Aiden, grinning and glancing at Sheryl, added, “Don’t worry, mate. We’ll keep you fed. Can’t wait for Minack Theatre, and by God’s grace, the weather is amazing today!”

Sheryl smiled softly, appreciating the group’s infectious excitement.

The drive to Perranporth was filled with laughter, the hum of their 90s playlist, and the occasional burst of spontaneous singing. Upon arrival, the expansive golden sands and the rhythmic crash of waves greeted them like an old friend.

“Race you to the water!” Aiden shouted, already charging toward the shoreline. The others laughed as they scrambled to keep up.

“He’s like a big kid, isn’t he?” Gareth said, smiling as he watched Aiden’s playful energy.

Sheryl watched him with a touch of admiration in her eyes, appreciating his carefree spirit.

After a leisurely stroll along the shore, collecting seashells and dipping their toes in the cool water, they made their way to the Perranzabuloe Museum. The quaint, volunteer-run museum offered a fascinating glimpse into the local history.

“Look at this old surfboard! Can you imagine using that?” Hannah marveled, running a hand over the vintage wood.

Gareth scoffed. “And this wool swimsuit? Must’ve been itchy as hell.”

Sheryl smiled, reading from a nearby placard. “It’s incredible how surfing has been part of Perranporth since the 1920s.”

Aiden raised an eyebrow with a grin. “Maybe we should’ve tried surfing here. Think they’d let us borrow these antiques?”

John chuckled. “Only if you promise not to break a hip, old man.”

The group laughed, then piled back into the car, spirits high after their beach stroll. The scenic drive along Cornwall’s rugged coastline provided plenty of photo ops and the occasional debate about the best 90s hits.

“Did you know Tintagel is linked to King Arthur? They say he was conceived there,” Hannah shared, eager to impress with her historical knowledge.

Gareth grimaced. “Conceived? That’s… more information than I needed.”

Aiden nudged him with a grin. “Come on, Gareth. Embrace the legend! Maybe you’ll find your inner knight.”

“If ‘inner knight’ gets a pint later, sure.”

As they approached Tintagel, the ruins perched dramatically on the cliffs, overlooking the tumultuous Atlantic below. It was a breathtaking sight, and they stood in awe, taking in the panoramic views.

John turned to the group. “Alright, kids! Remember, the site has steep slopes and uneven surfaces. Watch your step.”

Aiden grinned. “Thanks, Dad.”

Sheryl marvelled at the ruins. “It’s incredible to think this place dates back to the 13th century.”

Hannah, swept up in the romance of the place, sighed. “And the legends… imagine the stories these walls could tell.”

Gareth shook his head. “I’m more concerned about these 140 steep steps we have to climb. Whose idea was this again?”

John's grin widened. “Consider it your workout for the day, man.”

As they explored the ruins, their lighthearted banter continued. Sheryl found herself watching Aiden—his enthusiasm, his infectious energy, and the occasional glances he sent her way. It wasn’t lost on her that something was shifting, but she couldn’t quite put her finger on it.

Standing on the edge of the cliffs, the wind whipping through their hair, they felt connected not only to the past but to one another. It was a day of discovery—of history, yes, but also of the bonds forming between them.

John raised his arms to the sky. “Damn! This view is to die for.”

Gareth nodded. “I’ll admit, it’s pretty spectacular.”

Aiden grinned. “To new adventures and unforgettable memories.”

Sheryl added, “Cheers to that.”

After exploring the castle, the group’s stomachs began to growl. They decided to refuel at King Arthur’s Café, just a short walk from the castle grounds.

John stretched and rubbed his stomach. “I could go for a hearty Cornish pasty right about now.”

Hannah smiled. “And maybe some cream tea to satisfy my sweet tooth.”

They settled into a cosy corner and soon found themselves savoring traditional Cornish fare—flaky pasties and freshly baked scones, the perfect midday pick-me-up.

Aiden grinned as he dug into his pasty. “This clotted cream is something else. We need to bring some back.”

Gareth laughed. “If it survives the trip without being devoured.”

With their bellies full, they piled back into the car for the next leg of the journey—the Minack Theatre, an open-air amphitheatre carved into the cliffs overlooking the Atlantic.

John looked over at the group. “Let’s hit it! We’ve got a bit of a drive ahead. And then, the most awaited part of the trip.”

Hannah’s eyes sparkled. “Dibs on controlling the playlist!”

Gareth groaned. “No love songs.”

Aiden chuckled. “Oh, you heartbroken lil’ man. Some rock or pop, please.”

Sheryl smiled. “Sounds perfect.”

As they cruised through the scenic Cornish roads, the car was filled with pop music, laughter, and lighthearted debates about the best songs.

When they arrived at the Minack Theatre, they were greeted by breathtaking views of the ocean and the amphitheatre’s unique architecture. The venue, carved into the granite cliffs, offered a dramatic backdrop for the evening’s performance.

John marveled at the sight. “This place is incredible. Hard to believe it was all built by hand.”

Hannah nodded. “I read that a woman named Rowena Cade created it almost single-handedly.”

“Talk about dedication.” Gareth said soaking in the beauty of it all.

Aiden’s eyes shone. “I can’t wait to see the play here. It’s going to be epic.”

Sheryl, still in awe of the views, smiled. “Agreed. The setting alone is worth the trip.”

Aiden smiled at her. “You know, there’s a little garden area up top with even better views. Want to check it out before the play starts?”

The others waved him off, content to stay in their seats. Without hesitation, Sheryl shrugged, joining him. “Sure, why not?”

Aiden and Sheryl made their way up the stone steps, weaving through narrow pathways carved into the cliffside. The distant hum of the audience settling into their seats faded behind them, replaced by the rhythmic crash of waves far below. As they climbed higher, the air grew crisper, the salty scent of the sea filling their lungs.

When they reached the top, Sheryl stopped in her tracks.

The view was breathtaking.

The garden, built into the cliffside, was a wild contrast to the structured amphitheatre below. Vibrant subtropical plants framed the path, their deep greens and bright reds standing out against the golden hues of the setting sun. The ocean stretched endlessly before them, shimmering as the last light of day danced across its surface.

Aiden let out a low whistle. “Now this is a view.”

Sheryl nodded, stepping closer to the edge where a small stone railing separated them from the sheer drop to the waves below. “It’s unreal,” she murmured, wrapping her arms around herself as a cool breeze swept through.

Aiden glanced at her, his eyes glinting with something unreadable. “Cold?”

“I’m fine,” she said quickly, but before she could protest, he had already removed his lightweight jacket and draped it over her shoulders.

She blinked, surprised. “You didn’t have to—”

Aiden smirked. “I insist. Wouldn’t want you freezing before the play starts. I’d never hear the end of it.”

Sheryl rolled her eyes, but she didn’t shrug the jacket off. It felt warm, carrying the faint scent of sea salt and something distinctly *him*. They continued their walk, and Aiden slowed as he scanned the wooden plaques marking each raised flower bed—each one named after a Shakespearean character. His lips curled into a grin when he spotted one.

“Ah, look at this,” he said, motioning for Sheryl to come closer. “This one’s Juliet.”

Sheryl tilted her head, reading the inscription. “Makes sense,” she murmured, running her fingers over the delicate white flowers. Without thinking, she softly recited, *‘My bounty is as boundless as the sea, my love as deep.’*

Aiden raised an eyebrow. “For?”

It was only then that Sheryl realized what she had just said. She froze slightly, still standing near the raised bed, his jacket tucked over her shoulders, the wind brushing against her hair. Aiden’s gaze lingered on her.

Sheryl blinked. “What?”

Aiden leaned slightly closer; his voice laced with curiosity. “Your love as deep for whom?”

Her breath hitched. For a second, she panicked. Had she said too much? Did she make it sound like—

Shaking her head quickly, she shrugged. “Relax, Wynne. It’s just a quote from Juliet, for God’s sake.”

Aiden studied her, his teasing expression dimming slightly. “Hmm.”

Sheryl forced a laugh, trying to brush it off. “What?”

Aiden crossed his arms, watching her as if she’d almost given something away.

Before she could say anything more, a trumpet sounded, signalling that the play was about to start.

Sheryl took the moment to step away, clearing her throat. “We should head back before we miss the opening.”

Aiden hesitated for a beat, then nodded. “Right. Let’s go.”

As they made their way back down, the golden glow of the theatre lights came into view. Sheryl, her mind still racing, kept her head down, focusing on her steps. *Did I cross a line?*

No, of course not. It was just Shakespeare. Just a quote.

Nothing more.

Yet, as they took their seats beside each other, Aiden sitting close to her, his warmth just barely brushing against her side, Sheryl was still acutely aware that she was wearing his jacket.

And for some reason, she hadn’t taken it off.

Aiden, fully lost in the magic of the moment, was watching the play, his eyes wide with wonder. His dream—of being at Minack Theatre—was coming to life. His expression - pure contentment, the type of happiness that comes from knowing you’ve ticked off a bucket list item. Sheryl watched him with a quiet smile, her heart swelling a little, as she stole glances at him every now and then, noticing the light in his eyes, his joy as bright as the sky itself.

The play ended just when the sun was dipping low into the ocean. A perfect time for a “Dramatic ending”. The audience began to rise, but Aiden remained seated, entranced by the beauty surrounding him. The backdrop of the sky was a living painting—colours shifting, the ocean's roar in the distance, everything perfectly aligned as though the world had conspired to make this moment more than just a performance.

Sheryl lingered, waiting for him to come back to reality. Then, with a smile, Aiden stood up, still enchanted by what he had just witnessed. “It’s just… Amazing Sheryl.” And out of nowhere, he recited in a playful tone -

"Give me thy hand, and let me see thee in thy woman's weeds."

His words, though playful, hit something deeper within her. A warmth spread through Sheryl, and for a moment, she considered offering her hand to him. It was a simple gesture, a subtle invitation, but before she could act, the sharp ring of Aiden’s phone broke through the serene atmosphere. He glanced at the screen and picked it up almost immediately.

"Ciara.." his tone already shifting, he walked away from Sheryl without taking her hand, leaving her standing in the soft light of the setting sun, his jacket still draped over her shoulders. She watched him walk away, feeling the cool breeze against her skin.

The sunset was beautiful, now casting its fiery colours over the distant horizon, felt almost symbolic—her mind reminding her of Aiden’s words.. The sunset looked beautiful meaning letting someone go while still loving them from a distance.

Sheryl stood still, the weight of his jacket heavy on her shoulders, both comforting and burdensome all at once. Her heart ached, but there was something freeing in this moment too. She had wanted to offer her hand to him, had wanted to hold on, to keep that connection alive, but as she watched him fade into the distance, she knew, deep down, that this was the moment. She can’t keep clinging to something that isn’t hers to hold onto anymore.

The decision came quietly, like the final fade of the sun as it disappeared behind the waves. Let go. She needed to let him go.

As the sun finally set, casting the world in a soft, twilight glow, Sheryl took a deep breath, pulling Aiden’s jacket tighter around her. The warmth of it felt like a memory she wasn’t ready to release, but it was time she told herself. It was time to stop hurting herself for something that wasn't meant to be.

And with that, she pulled off his jacket from her shoulders and turned to join the others in the group as they traced their way back to the car park.
